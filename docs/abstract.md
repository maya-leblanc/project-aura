# abstract

Each year, thousands of individuals worldwide experience limb loss due to traumatic injury, diabetes-related complications, or war-related trauma. The loss of one or more fingers presents not only a functional deficit but also a profound psychological and emotional challenge. Tasks that were once automatic, buttoning a shirt, writing, or preparing food, can become daily obstacles. Traditional prosthetic solutions often include passive plastic limbs, which are lightweight, cost-effective, and shaped to resemble missing anatomy. While these devices offer no active movement, they provide psychological reassurance and help restore body image, which can positively impact social confidence and mental health.

However, passive prosthetics have well-documented limitations. Most are non-functional and unable to support fine motor control, active gripping, or adaptive responses to different tasks. They may also cause discomfort when worn for extended periods, particularly in hot or physically demanding environments. These shortcomings have prompted innovation in robotic prosthetics, technologies that incorporate sensors, actuators, and neural interfaces to restore more natural functionality.

To address the limitations of traditional prosthetics, Project AURA introduces a four-finger, EEG-controlled robotic hand designed specifically for individuals with partial hand amputations that exclude the thumb. By focusing on this amputation profile, the prosthetic meets the needs of a specific user group often underserved by full-hand prosthetics.

The system uses a non-invasive EEG headset to detect brain signals associated with motor intention. These signals are interpreted and processed in real-time by a custom-built microcontroller, which activates a series of high-torque servo motors. Each motor controls a tendon-like cable linked to one of the four prosthetic fingers, enabling individual finger articulation that mimics natural hand motion. The structure is housed in a lightweight, ergonomic 3D-printed frame, designed for daily use and long-term comfort.

To provide users with a sense of tactile interaction, force sensors are embedded at the tips of each finger. These sensors measure grip strength and relay the data to vibration motors embedded within the arm socket. This haptic feedback loop allows users to adjust grip pressure instinctively, even without visual cues, supporting safer manipulation of delicate or irregular objects. The prosthetic's control system maps trained EEG mental commands to distinct motion profiles such as pinching, grasping, and full-hand closure. No external apps or devices are required, as the signal processing, actuation control, and feedback systems are fully embedded within the unit.

Extended wear of prosthetic limbs introduces additional physical challenges related to environmental conditions. Excessive heat buildup inside the socket can lead to sweating, skin irritation, and reduced device stability. To mitigate this, the system integrates an environmental sensor to monitor internal and ambient temperature. Data from this sensor can inform adaptive responses such as cooling alerts or usage recommendations, helping prevent discomfort and maintaining consistent performance. This environmental awareness is particularly important for users in high-temperature climates or during active use.

Project AURA offers a practical, user-oriented advancement in robotic prosthetics by focusing on four-finger amputees, a group often overlooked in conventional prosthetic design. Through the integration of EEG-based control, finger-level actuation, sensory feedback, and environmental monitoring, this system restores not just movement, but autonomy and confidence. The project demonstrates how brain-computer interface technology can be translated into meaningful, real-world applications that improve quality of life for individuals living with limb loss.
