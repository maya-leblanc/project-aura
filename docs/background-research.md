# background-research

Limb loss affects millions of individuals globally, with finger and hand amputations being among the most common types, particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Amputation can occur due to various causes, including traumatic injury, peripheral vascular disease, diabetes mellitus, cancer, and war-related incidents [1][2]. In particular, diabetes-related complications are a leading cause of non-traumatic limb amputations worldwide, contributing to over 60% of lower-limb amputations in some regions [3]. Even the loss of a single digit can significantly disrupt a person’s ability to perform daily tasks, affecting autonomy, psychological well-being, and social identity.

Hand function is essential for a wide range of fine motor activities, writing, using tools, preparing food, or dressing oneself, and the absence of one or more fingers can result in decreased grip strength, impaired object manipulation, and reduced sensory feedback [4]. Beyond the physical limitations, there is a profound psychological dimension to amputation. Many amputees experience body image disturbance, social withdrawal, and depression, particularly when prosthetic options fail to meet their functional or emotional needs [5].

To address these challenges, passive cosmetic prosthetics are often prescribed. These devices are lightweight, cost-effective, and anatomically shaped to resemble the lost limb. While they offer minimal or no mechanical function, they serve a critical role in psychological rehabilitation, restoring a sense of wholeness and supporting social reintegration [6]. However, such prostheses are inherently limited. Most do not support active finger movement, grip modulation, or tactile feedback, and their utility declines over time as users seek more dynamic solutions [7].

These shortcomings have fueled innovation in the development of robotic and myoelectric prosthetics. Such devices are capable of replicating natural limb movement by responding to bioelectrical signals from residual muscles or the nervous system. Brain-computer interface (BCI) technologies, in particular, represent a cutting-edge approach in the field. By interpreting electroencephalography (EEG) signals directly from the brain, BCI systems allow users to control prosthetic devices with mental commands alone, offering a promising alternative for those with limited residual limb musculature or for whom myoelectric systems are not viable [8][9].

Project AURA was developed in response to these gaps, aiming to restore finger-level function in a subset of amputees with four-digit loss excluding the thumb. By focusing on this specific amputation profile, the project addresses a niche yet underserved population. The prosthetic system leverages non-invasive EEG signals to drive individual finger motion, integrating motor actuation, haptic feedback, and environmental monitoring to create a holistic, user-centered prosthetic solution.
